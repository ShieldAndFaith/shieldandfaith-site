Best Dog Crates and Gear for Large Breeds (2026 Guide)

S&F
Shield & Faith
April 21, 2026 · 13 min read

If you live with a big dog, you learn this fast: cheap gear doesn't stay cheap for long.

Large breeds don't just use dog gear harder — they stress test it. A flimsy crate becomes modern art by sunrise. A bargain bed gets flattened in a week. A weak leash clip mysteriously fails the exact moment your eighty-pound lunatic spots a squirrel.

We love big dogs, but they require gear that's built for real life. Especially in a busy first responder household where schedules are weird, people are in and out, and the dog needs safe structure even when life is chaotic.

This guide is built around that reality. These are the crates and gear pieces we'd actually trust for German Shepherds, Labs, Malinois, Goldens, heelers, and other big, strong, active dogs.

Why the Right Crate Matters

A crate isn't a punishment box. It's a safe place, a training tool, a travel solution, and sometimes the only reason your house is still standing when you leave for shift.

For big dogs, crate choice matters even more because size, strength, anxiety level, and chewing habits all change what works. A calm older Lab can do great in a standard wire crate. A young working-line Shepherd with separation anxiety can fold that same crate like a lawn chair.

The right crate should fit your dog, match their temperament, and give you peace of mind. That means looking at more than dimensions. Material, door design, airflow, portability, and how easy it is to clean all matter too.

Types of Crates for Large Dogs

Wire Crates

Great for calm dogs, general home use, airflow, and visibility. Usually the best value. Not ideal for escape artists.

Heavy-Duty Crates

Built for strong, anxious, or destructive dogs. More expensive, but sometimes cheaper than replacing three broken crates and your drywall.

Plastic / Travel Kennels

Good for travel, some airline use, and dogs that prefer a more den-like setup. Usually heavier and bulkier than wire crates.

Furniture-Style Crates

Best for dogs that are already crate trained and households that care about aesthetics. Not the move for a dog that treats wood like a snack.

How to Choose the Right Crate Size

Your dog should be able to stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably. That doesn't mean buying a crate so giant they can sprint laps inside it. Too much extra space can make house training harder and often just wastes room.

For a lot of large breeds, 42-inch or 48-inch crates are the sweet spot. If you're between sizes, go by your dog's height, length, and temperament — not just weight.

Frequently Asked Questions

What size crate does a German Shepherd need?

Most adult German Shepherds do best in a 42-inch or 48-inch crate depending on their size. Bigger males often need the 48-inch model for comfort.

Are wire crates good for large dogs?

Yes — if the dog is calm and crate trained. For anxious or destructive large dogs, heavy-duty steel or aluminum crates are usually safer and more durable.

What is the best crate for a dog with separation anxiety?

That depends on how severe the anxiety is. For dogs that break wire crates, heavy-duty options like Impact or ProSelect Empire are better choices. Just remember the crate doesn't solve anxiety by itself — training still matters.

Can large dogs sleep in crates every night?

Absolutely, as long as the crate is properly sized, comfortable, and introduced positively. Many dogs sleep better in a crate because it feels secure.

What gear is worth buying first for a large dog?

Start with a good crate, a quality bed, a sturdy leash/collar setup, food and water bowls that won't tip over, and some kind of vehicle restraint or travel solution.

Final Thoughts

Big dogs are worth every bit of chaos they bring into the house. But they need gear that matches their size, strength, and personality.

If your dog is calm, a good MidWest crate may be all you ever need. If your dog treats cheap crates like a personal challenge, skip the repeat purchases and buy stronger once. Either way, getting the right setup makes life easier for you and more secure for them.

And if you've ever watched a large dog fold a budget crate in half before breakfast, you already know: sometimes the expensive option is actually the cheaper one.
