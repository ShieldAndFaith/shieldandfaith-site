EDC Essentials: What Every Cop Actually Carries Off-Duty

S&F
Shield & Faith
April 6, 2026 · 4 min read

You're off the clock. You're at Target with your kid, grabbing diapers and a birthday card. And then something happens.

Every cop knows this scenario lives rent-free in their head. You never fully clock out — and your gear should reflect that. But here's the thing: off-duty carry isn't about hauling your patrol bag to the grocery store. It's about being prepared without looking like you're prepared.

Let's talk about what actually makes sense to carry when you're out of uniform.

The Off-Duty Mindset

First, real talk: there's a spectrum here.

Some guys carry almost nothing off-duty. Maybe a compact gun and their badge. Others basically strap on a mini patrol belt and call it casual. Most of us land somewhere in the middle — and where you land depends on your threat environment, your department's policies, and honestly, how much your back can take.

This isn't about judgment. It's about giving you a solid baseline to work from.

1. The Off-Duty Gun

This is the obvious one, but it's worth talking about because the gun you choose shapes everything else — holster, cover garment, mag carrier.

A lot of cops run a compact or subcompact version of their duty gun. There's logic to that: same manual of arms, same trigger, similar controls. Your hands already know what to do under stress.

Popular off-duty choices among the guys I know:

  • Glock 43/43X/48
  • Sig P365 or P365XL
  • Smith & Wesson Shield Plus
  • Glock 19 (for those who don't mind printing a little)

Whatever you carry, carry it in a quality holster that covers the trigger guard, retains the gun, and lets you draw consistently. A $15 Amazon special isn't it.

2. A Reload

One mag. That's all. You're not clearing a building — you're buying time to get to your car or get your family to cover.

A spare mag in a pocket or on a dedicated carrier gives you options. Tuck it in a front pocket. Get a small mag carrier that fits inside the waistband. Just have it.

👉 Search for concealed carry magazine carriers on Amazon

3. A Tourniquet

This one's non-negotiable for me, and it should be for you too.

A CAT or SOFTT-W tourniquet takes up almost no space and can save your life or someone else's. Shove it in a cargo pocket, a fanny pack, or a dedicated pouch on your belt. Just get it on your person.

We've all worked scenes where someone bled out waiting for EMS. Don't let that be you or someone you love.

👉 Search for CAT tourniquets on Amazon

4. A Light

A good flashlight is one of those things where once you start carrying one, you can't believe you ever didn't. Not just for tactical situations — for looking under your car, finding your keys in a dark parking garage, checking that weird sound in the backyard at 2am.

You don't need your full-size Streamlight off-duty. Something compact but bright — like the Streamlight MicroStream or the ThruNite T1 — fits in a pocket and throws enough light to matter.

If your off-duty gun has a rail and you're running it in certain environments, a weapon-mounted light on the gun is worth considering too.

👉 Search for compact EDC flashlights on Amazon

5. A Knife

A knife earns its place in EDC for two reasons: utility and defense.

The utility case is obvious — boxes, zip ties, seatbelts. The defensive case is for situations where a firearm isn't accessible or appropriate, or as a backup.

A good mid-range folder — Benchmade, Spyderco, Kershaw — runs $50-150 and will last years. Clip it to your pocket and forget about it until you need it.

👉 Search for EDC folding knives on Amazon

6. Your Badge and ID

Seems obvious, right? But you'd be amazed how many guys leave the house without their badge when they're off-duty.

If you get involved in an incident, your badge and department ID are how responding units know you're the good guy. Carry them. Every time.

A slim leather badge holder that fits in your back pocket or inside a jacket works great and doesn't add bulk.

👉 Search for slim leather badge holders on Amazon

7. Cell Phone (Charged)

Not gear per se, but don't overlook it. Your phone is how you call for backup, document scenes, and coordinate with incoming units. Keep it charged. Consider a small battery pack in your bag or car if you're prone to running low.

The "It Depends" Items

These are things some guys carry that make a lot of sense depending on your situation:

OC Spray — Great for de-escalation without going lethal. Compact units like Sabre Red fit in a pocket. Worth it if your department allows it off-duty.

👉 Search for compact OC spray on Amazon

Individual First Aid Kit (IFAK) — If you're carrying a bag (backpack, range bag, diaper bag), throw an IFAK in it. Tourniquet, chest seal, pressure bandage. You're already trained on this stuff.

👉 Search for compact IFAKs on Amazon

Handcuffs — Controversial. Takes up space and weight. But if you work a high-crime area or your off-duty habits take you into sketchy environments, having a pair in your car or bag isn't a bad call.

👉 Search for handcuffs on Amazon

Making It Work Every Day

Here's the honest truth about EDC: the best setup is the one you'll actually carry.

A full-size gun and a complete IFAK are objectively more capable than a pocket pistol and nothing else. But if the full setup is so uncomfortable or noticeable that you leave it home half the time, it's not serving you.

Start with your gun and a reload. Add a light and a tourniquet. Build from there based on what you'll realistically have on you every day.

Your family doesn't care how tactical your setup is. They care that you come home — and that you can protect them if things go sideways in the cereal aisle.

Stay safe out there.
