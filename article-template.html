The 5 Pieces of Duty Gear That Actually Made a Difference

S&F
Shield & Faith
March 28, 2026 · 8 min read

When you strap on a duty belt for the first time, everything feels important. Every pouch, every holster, every clip. But after a few years on the street, you start to realize that most gear is just okay — and a few pieces are genuinely life-changing.

This isn't a sponsored post. Nobody paid me to write this. These are the five pieces of gear that earned their place on my belt (and in my bag) through years of patrol, investigations, and everything in between.

1. A Quality Duty Flashlight

This might sound basic, but hear me out. Your flashlight is arguably the most-used tool on your belt after your radio. A cheap light that flickers, dims, or dies at the wrong moment isn't just inconvenient — it's dangerous.

"The first time my light died during a building search, I upgraded the next day. Some lessons only need to happen once."

I've gone through more flashlights than I can count. The ones that survive are the ones built with quality LED emitters, solid aluminum bodies, and rechargeable batteries that actually hold a charge through a 12-hour shift. Look for at least 1,000 lumens with a good throw pattern and a tail switch you can activate with gloves on.

2. Proper Boots That Support Your Feet

Your feet carry you through every shift. Bad boots mean bad knees, bad back, and bad morale. I spent my first two years in whatever the department issued, and my body paid for it.

The upgrade to quality tactical boots with proper arch support and waterproofing was night and day. Look for:

  • Composite or no safety toe (lighter weight for patrol)
  • Waterproof membrane — not water-resistant, waterproof
  • Quality insole or room for custom orthotics
  • Side zip for quick on/off
  • Slip-resistant outsole rated for multiple surfaces

Your feet will thank you. Your back will thank you. Your spouse will thank you when you stop complaining.

3. A Reliable Backup Communication Method

Radio systems fail. Batteries die. Dead zones exist. Having a reliable backup — whether it's a secondary radio, a quality phone mount in your patrol car, or a personal locator — isn't paranoia. It's preparation.

I keep a compact secondary radio charged and ready, plus a magnetic phone mount rated for pursuit-speed driving. The mount alone has paid for itself in convenience and safety more times than I can count.

4. Quality Body Armor That Actually Fits

Most departments issue body armor. Most of it fits terribly. Getting your vest properly fitted — or investing in a quality carrier system — makes the difference between wearing it reluctantly and wearing it comfortably for 12 hours.

"Armor only works if you wear it. And you'll only wear it consistently if it's comfortable enough to forget it's there."

Key things to look for in a carrier upgrade:

  • Breathable mesh backing with moisture-wicking material
  • Adjustable shoulder and side straps for a custom fit
  • MOLLE webbing for modular gear attachment
  • Load-bearing design that distributes weight to your hips

5. A Purpose-Built Duty Notebook System

This one surprises people, but a solid note-taking system is critical gear. Not your phone's notes app — an actual, write-anywhere, court-admissible notebook system.

I use a waterproof notepad with a dedicated pen that writes in rain, upside down, and on wet surfaces. It lives in a purpose-built pouch on my vest. Every call, every contact, every detail — written down in the moment.

This has saved me in court testimony, internal reviews, and my own memory more times than any tactical gadget ever has. When the lawyer asks "And how do you recall that detail, officer?" — you hold up the notebook.

The Bottom Line

Good gear doesn't have to be expensive gear, but it does have to be intentional. Buy what solves real problems, test it before you trust your life to it, and don't be afraid to upgrade when something isn't working.

Your gear is part of your safety system. Treat it that way.
